Lesson 23 Court Precedents

Review modern legal and justice structures. Click on highlighted collocations to pull up official legal files.

The government is responsible for passing laws, and the courts and legal system are in charge of upholding and enforcing the law.

When a person is charged with a crime, he or she must appear in court to stand trial. Both the prosecution and the defense will present their cases before the judge and jury. After careful consideration, the judge or jury will reach a verdict.

If there is irrefutable, overwhelming, or concrete evidence, this means that it is very obvious that the suspect committed the crime, and it is highly possible that the jury's verdict will be a unanimous decision. However, if the evidence is circumstantial or inconclusive evidence, then the decision will be more difficult — it is even possible to have a hung jury, which cannot agree on the verdict.

Less serious crimes will receive a light sentence — for example, the offender may have to pay a fine or do community service. For more serious crimes, the person will probably go to prison. Especially heinous crimes can result in a harsh sentence such as life in prison or, in some places, the death penalty.

After the decision is made, the lawyers can choose to appeal the verdict, and the case may turn into a prolonged legal battle as the courts decide whether to uphold the verdict or overturn the verdict.

As a convicted criminal is serving time, they should maintain good behavior for the possibility of being released early, as long as they are not deemed a threat to society. A criminal released early is said to be on parole, meaning they must strictly comply with certain conditions.
