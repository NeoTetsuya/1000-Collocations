Lesson 13 Narrative

Explore education collocations. Click any highlighted expression below to study its context.

Most children begin their studies at 5 or 6 years old in pre-school, followed by kindergarten. After that comes primary / elementary school, which consists of first grade through fifth grade. Then there's middle school and high school.

After high school, you apply to colleges / universities to see which ones will accept you. You send in / submit your application and then wait for the results. If you got good grades in high school, you might get / be awarded a scholarship to help pay the tuition.

In college, you'll take classes / courses on various subjects. You should always take notes as the teacher is giving the lecture. Some teachers may ask the students to give / make presentations or do research and write an essay, an assignment, a report, or a paper. After you hand in your work, the teacher will grade the assignment and give you feedback.

To evaluate your progress, you'll take tests / exams. If you get the minimum number of points required, you've passed the test; if you don't get the minimum number of points, you've failed the test. It's also possible to pass / fail a course.

If the course proves to be too difficult, or if you don't like it, you can withdraw from or drop the course.

Most college students choose a major / concentration. To talk about a concentration of studies, you can use major as a verb or a noun – for example, "I majored in biology" or "I was a biology major." When you graduate from college, you'll get a diploma. Afterwards, you can say you have a degree in the area of your concentration, such as a bachelor's degree, master's degree, or doctorate.
