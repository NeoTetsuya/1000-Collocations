When a person breaks the law, we say they commit a crime. Especially horrible crimes, like a savage rape or a brutal murder, can be described as barbaric crimes.

If someone attempts to commit a crime but does not succeed, we use the word attempted. If someone accuses another person of a crime but it is not yet proven, then we use the word alleged. After the victim reports the crime to the police, the police will investigate the crime.

They’ll visit the crime scene and gather forensic evidence. If witnesses come forward, the police will interview them to hear a first-hand account of what happened. They also consider possible motives for the crime.

Police can arrest suspects, but the suspect can prove their innocence by providing a solid alibi. If it is never discovered who committed the crime, then the case remains an unsolved crime. The statistics about the number of crimes in a particular area is called the crime rate, and a big increase in crime can be called a crime wave.

There are also a number of collocations we can use to describe criminals. A first-time offender is someone who has broken the law for the first time, whereas someone who has broken the law in the past has a criminal record. A criminal who is famous, like a serial killer or someone involved in organized crime, can be called a notorious criminal. Other specific crime collocations include domestic violence, drug trafficking, identity theft, sexual harassment, petty crime, and armed robbery.
