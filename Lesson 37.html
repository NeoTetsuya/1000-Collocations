The Dialogue Protocol

Harness conversational etiquette, tone, and critiques. Click highlighted collocations to pull up definitions.

To become a better communicator, you must learn to make small talk easily. When you meet new colleagues, look for a natural moment to strike up a conversation. You can safely make comments about your environment or the weather.

However, try to steer clear of delicate subjects like personal finances, politics, or beliefs. You don't want to drag someone into a lengthy discussion that might end in tension, or worse, cause you to have an argument that spoils the mood.

If there is ever a lull in the conversation, gracefully ask the other person about their day or hobbies. When telling a personal anecdote, avoid the temptation to go on and on about yourself. Remember, you are just having a brief chat; you are not trying to make a speech to an assembly!

Be particularly careful when cracking jokes, as misunderstandings happen quickly. If someone takes offense, you must step forward and apologize profusely. Outstanding communication skills revolve around knowing how to get straight to the point cleanly instead of dropping hints and expecting people to guess.

It's equally important to build trust. Never tell lies, and resist the urge to stretch the truth to sound more impressive. Always tell the truth so that when you give your word, people know they can rely on you.

Finally, pay attention to your tone of voice. Sarcasm or sarcastic remarks can erode friendships, while disparaging comments will only alienate those around you. If you have to make a complaint, frame it as constructive criticism, and whenever you ask a favor, always speak with politeness.

Linguistic Physics

The vocabulary of interpersonal speaking is strictly bound by natural formulas. We **apologize profusely** rather than 'sorry highly', and we **stretch the truth** rather than 'grow the facts'. Master these exact patterns to elevate your speaking and writing competence.
