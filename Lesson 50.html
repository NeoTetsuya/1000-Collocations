To finish up our study of collocations, we are going to focus on expressions with everyday verbs. A lot of learners make mistakes here because these pairings vary significantly in other languages. Consider children: we say a mother will have a baby, and couples hope to have children. When going out, we want to have a blast, have fun, or simply have a good time. We also use have for meals: to have breakfast, lunch, dinner, or a snack. In our career journeys, we must be ready to have an opportunity which allows us to have an advantage.

Next are expressions with 'take'. Every morning, it is standard to take a shower or take a bath. At school, students will take a class, then take a test or exam. For travel, we take a bus, train, or taxi. When exhausted, we take a break or take a rest to pause our work. If a golden chance appears, we must take advantage of the moment, ensure we take care of details, and take action immediately.

Crucially, some collocations with 'pay' have nothing to do with money. We must pay attention to lectures, pay tribute to heroes, or pay someone a compliment to lift spirits. You can also pay someone a visit informally, or pay your respects at a memorial.

To change our behavior, we try to break a habit, and must never break a promise. At social events, we break the ice to warm up quiet groups. Those who break the rules will face discipline. And when conveying difficult updates, we have to break the news gently.

In everyday incidents, we might catch a cold or catch the flu. If wood gets too close to heat, it will catch fire. During travels, we might catch a glimpse of wild animals, or try to catch someone's eye to connect. Finally, we might sprint to catch the bus or subway to head home.

Policies eventually come into force or come to an effect. All semesters must come to an end. Then we must come to terms with our results, especially when our actions come under scrutiny or when we come close to full marks. Finally, we use 'get' for changes: to get married, get divorced, get pregnant, or get angry, tired, or excited.

Linguistic Laws of Verbs
