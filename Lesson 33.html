The Startup Crossroads

Explore precise decisions and complex options. Click highlighted collocations to pull up decision cards.

As the founder of a promising clean-tech venture, I had to make a decision that would define our legacy. Our lead scientist presented a brilliant algorithm, but incorporating it required us to choose between two divergent routes: scale organically or seek venture capital. To make a choice of this magnitude, I needed to consider our options carefully and thoroughly weigh the pros and cons.

It was an incredibly tough decision. Our investors urged me to make up my mind, but I preferred to keep our options open while the board evaluated potential partners. Our analyst compiled a wide range of options, and we managed to narrow down our options to a select few. We decided to draw up a shortlist of high-profile impact funds.

I was determined to make an informed decision rather than committing to a snap decision. However, when our primary competitor unexpectedly launched a clone, I faced a forced choice. We had no choice but to act immediately. During a tense morning board meeting, I made a split-second decision to reject their acquisition bid. We chose to run the risk of a hostile buyout and stand our ground.

Many industry journals criticized our stubbornness, calling our stance an arbitrary decision. But I had to defend my decision in front of our stakeholders. I resolved to stick to my decision because I believed in our team\'s mission. It was a wise decision. We built our framework entirely by choice, and I knew that if we remained patient and learned to choose wisely, we would scale successfully in the long run.

Lexical Decisiveness

English maps descriptions of choices to exact, fixed combinations. You **make up your mind** rather than 'arrange' it. You make a **snap decision** rather than a 'fast decision'. Avoid incorrect pairings to sound fully natural.
