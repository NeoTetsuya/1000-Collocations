The word "texture" refers to the way something feels, and there are many different collocations used to describe it. Let's look at them in pairs of opposites.

First, let's look at dry hair versus oily hair or greasy hair. You need to wash oily hair more often than you need to wash dry hair. We also distinguish between dry skin and oily skin. Oily skin is prone to acne, whereas dry skin often needs moisturizer.

Surfaces such as the ground, a road, and your skin can be a smooth surface (with a consistent surface) or a rough surface (with a varied surface).

The water of the ocean is calm water when the weather is mild, and it is choppy water or rough water when there are high winds. A mattress or pillow, for example, can be a hard object or a soft object.

Meat that is tender meat is easy to cut and chew, and meat that is tough meat is difficult to cut and chew. A knife or a pencil can have a sharp blade (meaning it can cut easily) or a dull blade (meaning it has lost its ability to cut).

The verbs soften and harden describe the process of becoming soft and becoming hard. For example, potatoes soften when you cook them in boiling water, while snow hardens if the temperature decreases and the snow begins turning into ice.

We can also use "soften" and "harden" in a more metaphorical way - we can say a person's voice softens when they begin speaking in a more friendly tone, and their voice hardens when they begin speaking in a less friendly tone. A person's attitude softens (becomes more friendly/tolerant) or attitude hardens (becomes less friendly/less tolerant).

Texture words are often used when describing food. The word creamy describes a smooth and rich texture, like cream, typical for a creamy alfredo sauce, soup, pudding, or chocolate. Foods that make a noise when you chew them can be described as crunchy or crispy (like crackers, chips, crispy bacon, or granola). Caramels are chewy, which can be negative when meat is overcooked and becomes tough and chewy. Finally, greasy describes oily food, such as a greasy hamburger, greasy French fries, or chicken wings.

Tactile Boundaries

English separates textures clearly. **Water** is described as **calm** or **choppy**, while **meat** is classified as **tender** or **tough**. **Blades** can be **sharp** or **dull**. Metaphorically, a person's voice or attitude can **soften** or **harden** over time.
