The word cause is primarily used with negative results. A storm can cause damage, a mechanical defect can cause a problem, unclear instructions can cause confusion, a political scandal can cause an uproar, a high-risk sport can cause injury, and an infection in a hospital can cause death.

You can describe something as cause for concern or cause for alarm, meaning a reason for concern or alarm. For example, the growing number of teenagers dropping out of school is cause for concern. The word alarm is a little stronger and more urgent; the rapid spread of a virus is cause for alarm. One of the only positive collocations is cause for celebration. The opening of the new school was cause for celebration in the community.

When analyzing a problem, you can identify the main cause of the issue, but keep in mind that there may be underlying causes as well. The deepest, most fundamental trigger is sometimes called the root cause. Interestingly, we do not use the verb cause together with effect. Instead, we say that something has an effect. For example, television has an effect on public opinion. Another way to say a strong effect is a significant impact or a profound impact.

There are many ways to describe effects: short-term effects vs. long-term effects, visible effects vs. subtle effects, powerful effect vs. minimal effect, beneficial effect vs. adverse effect, and main effect vs. side effect.

Something can also have or produce results. Results that obviously exist and can be proved are called concrete results, and a result that is impossible to avoid is an inevitable result. Results can also be direct results or indirect, and the outcome you want is the desired result. One similar term is outcome; you can guess the likely outcome, expect the logical outcome, or face an unexpected outcome.
