Today you're going to expand your vocabulary by learning various collocations about colors! You can describe a color as light or dark—for example, someone can have light brown or dark brown hair. If a color is very intense, you can describe it as bright—for example, a bright red apple. If the color is not very intense, you can describe it as pale—for example, a pale green dress.

When describing colors in general, we talk about intense, bold/vivid colors, or light, delicate pastel colors. Designers utilize diverse objects to describe specific shades of a color. Our studio exhibits gems of ruby red and cherry red, paintings of a fiery orange sunset, and autumn leaves of burnt orange.

We catalog vibrant lemon yellow and golden yellow, contrasting them with rich emerald green and organic olive green. For skies, we use a light shade like sky blue, transitioning to deep royal blue, or a dark navy blue / midnight blue.

When you have something that is primarily one color, but with a little bit of another color included, we can use the word "tinge". You might describe white flowers with a tinge of pink, or a light gray fabric with a slight brownish tinge. Combining color bases with "-ish" describes blended shades, such as clay with a reddish brown hue or sand that appears yellowish brown.

In wardrobe styling, some colors look good together—in this case, we say the colors match. Other colors look terrible together—in this case, we say the colors clash. Choosing shades that match a person's skin tone is vital—if a garment looks good, you can say "that color suits you" or "that color looks good on you."

Finally, a tip about changing the color of something. We use the verb "paint" in most cases—you paint a house and paint your nails. But we use the verb "dye" for hair and cloth/fabric—so you dye your hair blonde and dye a shirt blue!

Lexical Precision

Color descriptions in English follow exact collocational patterns. You **dye** cloth, fabric, and hair, but you **paint** houses and nails. Some colors **match** beautifully, while others **clash** terribly. This design lab secures these standard pairings directly in your brain.
